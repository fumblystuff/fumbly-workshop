A friend of mine asked me to help him create a seating chart stand for his daughter's wedding. The job required that we cut a 4x8 foot piece of plywood to 3 feet wide then cut an arc across the top. Here's a photo of the final product:

<!-- ![A photo of the finished seating chart stand](/images/2026/circle-cutting-jig-00.jpg) -->

To cut the top arc, I needed a good circle cutting jig. I didn't want to make one, mostly because I'm often lazy and there were a lot of options available as commercial products. I decided to purchase the [Rockler Circle Cutting Jig for Routers](https://www.rockler.com/rockler-circle-cutting-jig){target="_blank"}. 

## Three Options

Rockler offers multiple circle cutting jigs:

+ [Rockler Circle Cutting Jig for Routers](https://www.rockler.com/rockler-circle-cutting-jig){target="_blank"}
+ [Rockler Ellipse/Circle Cutting Jig for Routers](https://www.rockler.com/rockler-circle-ellipse-router-jig){target="_blank"}
+ [Rockler Ellipse/Circle Cutting Jig for Compact Routers](https://www.rockler.com/rockler-ellipse-circle-cutting-jig-for-compact-routers){target="_blank"}

The ellipse/circle cutting jigs are cool (and interesting) because they allow you to cut both circles as well as ellipses, but since this was my first foray into circle/arc cutting, I didn't want to do anything complicated. I felt that jumping to the ellipse versions would make it more difficult for me to learn how to use them.
